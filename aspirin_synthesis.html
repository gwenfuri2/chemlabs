Aspirin Synthesis

1. Purpose

Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) is synthesized through an esterification reaction involving salicylic acid and acetic anhydride in the presence of an acid catalyst. The goal is to observe the formation of aspirin and evaluate its purity through melting point analysis and the ferric chloride test.

2. Theory

The hydroxyl (-OH) group of salicylic acid reacts with acetic anhydride, catalyzed by sulfuric or phosphoric acid, to form the ester acetylsalicylic acid. This is a common organic chemistry example of a nucleophilic acyl substitution mechanism.

4. Procedure

Step 1: Add Salicylic Acid

Salicylic acid is added to an empty flask to begin the synthesis.

Salicylic acid is the starting material that will be acetylated to form aspirin. Precise weighing ensures accurate stoichiometry.

Step 2: Add Acetic Anhydride & H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ

Reagents are added from a cartoon dropper into the flask.

Acetic anhydride is the acetylating agent, and sulfuric acid acts as a catalyst to increase the reaction rate by protonating the carbonyl oxygen.

Step 3: Heat Reaction Mixture

The flask is placed into a warm water bath and the temperature begins to rise.

Heating the reaction speeds up ester formation and allows the reaction to proceed toward completion.

Step 4: Cool in Ice Bath

The flask is placed into an ice bath and begins to cool as water is slowly added.

Cooling and adding water quenches the reaction and precipitates the aspirin product out of the solution.

Step 5: Crystal Formation

As the solution cools, aspirin crystals begin to form and settle at the bottom of the flask.

Crystallization separates the solid aspirin from the liquid mixture. Filtration allows for isolation and drying of the product.

Step 6: Weigh Crystals

The collected aspirin crystals are transferred to a scale for weighing and observation.

Weighing helps determine the yield. Observing color/texture helps assess product quality and potential impurities.

Step 7: Ferric Chloride Test

Ferric chloride is added to each sample to check for unreacted salicylic acid.

The FeCl‚ÇÉ test detects unreacted salicylic acid by producing a violet complex with phenol groups. A light color suggests higher purity.
